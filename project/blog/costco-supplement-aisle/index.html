The 'proprietary blend' is the cleanest tax on the people who can't afford a chemist.

Brands hide doses behind the phrase "proprietary blend" because they want to charge clinical-grade prices for sub-clinical doses. The math behind why it works — with fourteen pre-workout labels we ran through the lab last month.

There is a phrase printed on the side of more than half the products we test, and the phrase is "proprietary blend." It is — and I say this with the full benefit of a doctorate in pharmacology — a euphemism. It exists for one reason: to let a brand charge you the price of a clinical dose for an amount of active ingredient that wouldn't move a needle in a calibrated assay, much less a calibrated body.

Here is what the FDA permits. A supplement label must disclose every ingredient in the product. It must disclose the total amount of any "proprietary blend." It does not have to disclose how much of each ingredient is in the blend. So you get a label that looks like this: "Pump & Performance Matrix™ 4,500mg — L-Citrulline Malate, Beta-Alanine, Betaine, Taurine, L-Tyrosine, Caffeine Anhydrous."

It looks legitimate. There are five clinically-validated compounds named. There is a number — 4,500mg — that sounds substantial. And the label is, technically, compliant. The problem is that L-Citrulline alone requires 6,000–8,000mg to produce any of the vascular effects the marketing copy describes. So at most you are getting a third of the citrulline dose at most a fifth of the beta-alanine dose, and so on down to caffeine — which, since brands know consumers can feel caffeine, is usually loaded at the top of the blend and is the only ingredient at a real dose.

01 — DEFINITIONWhat a proprietary blend actually is

A proprietary blend is a list of ingredients with a single combined weight. The FDA requires the ingredients to be listed in descending order by weight. Beyond that, no disclosure is required.

So if a blend is "5,000mg of Citrulline, Beta-Alanine, Betaine, Taurine, Caffeine" you know that there is more citrulline than beta-alanine, more beta-alanine than betaine, more betaine than taurine, and more taurine than caffeine. You do not know the amounts. You do not know whether the citrulline is 4,900mg with the other four ingredients at 25mg each — a label that is technically compliant and pharmacologically a citrulline-only product.

The proprietary blend is to ingredient disclosure what a hot dog is to meat labeling: technically descriptive, morally hostile. — A pharmacist friend, off the record

02 — ECONOMICSThe cost math, with numbers

Here's the price of the active ingredients at industrial scale, sourced from industry-rate bulk material indices. These are the prices brands pay per kilogram before formulation and packaging:

Per clinical serving · raw material cost

L-Citrulline at clinical dose (6g): $0.28. Beta-Alanine at clinical dose (3.2g): $0.09. Creatine Mono (5g): $0.06. Caffeine (200mg): $0.003. The active ingredients of a fully-dosed pre-workout: under fifty cents.

$0.42raw

That is the wholesale cost, at clinical doses, of the actives in a properly-dosed pre-workout. The retail price of a "clinical-grade" pre-workout, on average, is $1.93 per serving (n=86 products, Fitlab Q1 2026 dataset). The difference covers packaging, distribution, marketing, and — substantially — margin.

This is fine. It is fine that a brand makes a margin on actives that cost forty cents and sell for two dollars. What is not fine is when the same brand sells $1.93 per serving of under-dosed actives — citrulline at 1.8g, beta-alanine at 0.9g, creatine at 1.2g — and pretends the dosing is clinical because the math is hidden behind a proprietary blend.

04 — REBUTTALThe defense, and why it fails

Industry's defense of proprietary blends comes in two flavors:

  1. Trade secret protection. If we disclosed our doses, competitors would copy our formula. Counter: dose ratios are not patentable; the ingredients are listed on the label anyway. A competitor with a fifty-dollar lab budget can reverse-engineer the formula in a week. The "trade secret" defense protects against consumers, not competitors.
  2. Formulation synergy. Our doses are calibrated to work in combination, even at lower individual amounts. Counter: this is a testable claim. We have not seen a single brand publish the human trial data on their "synergistic blend." When asked, all eleven prop-blend brands declined to comment.

05 — PRACTICALWhat to do

Three things:

  • Never buy a pre-workout that lists a proprietary blend. Full disclosure should be a hard cut. If the brand won't tell you the dose, the brand is telling you the dose isn't there.
  • Compute cost-per-clinical-serving, not cost-per-scoop. We have a calculator. The 'cheap' pre-workout is often the most expensive on this metric.
  • Read the certifications. NSF, Informed Sport, and USP Verified all require dose disclosure. A certified product with a prop blend on the label is mathematically impossible.

06 — CODAWhy this matters

Supplements are an asymmetric market. Brands know exactly what's in the bottle; consumers do not. Proprietary blends are not a regulatory grey area — they are the loudest piece of evidence that the asymmetry exists by design. If you take only one thing from this piece: the brands that hide their doses are not protecting trade secrets. They are protecting the price.
