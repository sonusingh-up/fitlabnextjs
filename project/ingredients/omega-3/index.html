Creatine monohydrate works. It increases muscle phosphocreatine stores, which buffer ATP regeneration during ≤30-second maximal efforts. The effect is well-replicated for strength, sprint performance, and (to a slightly lesser extent) lean body mass.

The dose is 3–5 g/day, taken any time of day. A "loading phase" of 20 g/day for 5–7 days accelerates saturation by ~3 weeks but offers no additional ceiling. The performance gain from loading versus maintenance is within ±2% by week 4 — see our meta-analysis.

Monohydrate is the form to use. Creapure® (AlzChem GmbH) is the gold-standard supplier. HCl, ethyl ester, and "buffered" forms claim superior absorption; none has demonstrated meaningfully different muscle PCr saturation at equivalent doses. They cost 2–4× more for the same effect.

  • Use it if
    You train at high intensity, sprint, or want to gain lean mass.
  • Skip if
    You have stage 3+ CKD; rare GI sensitivity at 5g+.

  • Avoid forms

02 WHAT IT ACTUALLY DOES

The mechanism, simplified.

Creatine doesn't make you stronger directly — it gives your muscle cells a faster way to regenerate ATP. The energy substrate matters more than the load you can lift in any one rep; saturation enables a higher work-rate ceiling across the workout.

During short, maximal efforts (≤30 sec), muscle cells consume ATP faster than they can produce it aerobically. The phosphocreatine (PCr) system bridges this gap: PCr donates a phosphate to ADP, regenerating ATP within milliseconds.

Supplementing creatine raises the size of the available PCr pool by ~20%. The result: more ATP regenerations per unit time, which translates to higher work-rate ceiling, more reps before failure, and faster between-set recovery.

This mechanism only matters for ATP-PCr-limited efforts. Endurance running, low-intensity steady-state cardio, and rest-day cognition do not benefit. See the meta-analysis for the pooled effect-size breakdown by exercise modality.

06 SIDE EFFECTS & INTERACTIONS

What the studies don't put on the bottle.

✓Generally safe

  • SAFETY · 30+ YR
    Renal markers unchanged across long-term studies up to 21 months at 5g/day. Healthy kidneys handle creatine load without issue.
  • WATER RETENTION
    Intracellular only. The "creatine bloat" myth conflates intramuscular water (desirable for performance) with subcutaneous water (not what's happening).
  • CRAMPING
    Not increased in studies. 30+ trials specifically tracked cramping; rates were equal or lower vs. placebo. The anecdote does not survive RCT.
  • PUBERTY
    Safe in adolescents per ISSN. Used in pediatric muscle-wasting disorders at higher doses without adverse signal.

!Watch for

  • GI · 6%
    Mild GI upset in ~6% of users at 5g+ single dose. Resolves by splitting dose or taking with food.
  • CKD STAGE 3+
    Avoid unless cleared. Compromised kidneys cannot handle the creatinine output load. Stage 1–2 generally fine; confirm with nephrology.
  • SCr LAB
    Elevates serum creatinine 0.1–0.3 mg/dL. Not kidney injury — a metabolic artifact of higher PCr turnover. Flag to your physician before bloodwork.
  • CAFFEINE
    Timing interaction — co-ingestion may blunt creatine's performance effect (Vandenberghe 1996). Take creatine and pre-workout caffeine ≥1 hour apart.

08 — COMMON ERRORS

What people get wrong about creatine.

01
Skipping it because of "water retention"

Wrong target. The water is intracellular and improves performance. Subcutaneous water is not affected by creatine intake at clinical doses. Scale weight goes up; visible bloat does not.

02
Loading because "fast-acting is better"

It isn't. 20g/d for 5 days gets you to saturation 3 weeks sooner. The performance ceiling is identical by week 4 — there's no efficacy advantage, only timing. GI side-effects are also concentrated in the loading phase.

03
Paying $40+/month for "premium" forms

You're funding marketing. HCl, buffered, magnesium chelate, ethyl ester — none has demonstrated equivalent or superior PCr saturation vs monohydrate at equal dose. Monohydrate at $12/month is the right answer.

04
Cycling on/off (8 weeks on, 4 off)

Pointless. Endogenous creatine synthesis is suppressed during supplementation and rebounds within 2 weeks of cessation. Cycling provides no physiological benefit; it just temporarily reduces your performance ceiling.

05
Stopping pre-blood-test

Inform the lab instead. Serum creatinine will read 0.1–0.3 mg/dL higher than baseline. This is metabolic spillover from elevated PCr turnover, not kidney injury. A two-week washout normalizes the value.

06
Taking it pre-workout with caffeine

Timing matters. Vandenberghe 1996 found caffeine blunted creatine's performance effect when co-ingested at high doses. The mechanism is uncertain but the signal replicates. Take creatine off-workout days; or ≥1 hour from caffeine.

11 FREQUENTLY ASKED

The questions our readers actually ask.

Do I need to "load" creatine for it to work?
No. Loading at 20 g/day for 5–7 days accelerates muscle saturation by ~3 weeks but does not raise the steady-state ceiling. 3–5 g/day for 28 days reaches the same saturation level. If you're starting today and have a competition in 10 days, load. Otherwise, don't bother — and avoid the GI side-effects that the loading phase concentrates. See our pooled analysis.
Will creatine make me look bloated?
No — visibly. Creatine increases intracellular water (inside the muscle cell), which improves cell volume and performance. It does not increase subcutaneous water, which is what causes visible "bloat". Scale weight goes up 1–2 kg in the first 4 weeks; visible body composition is unchanged or improves.
Is it safe for my kidneys?
Yes, for healthy kidneys. 30+ years of safety data including studies up to 21 months at 5 g/day. Stage 3+ CKD is a contraindication — compromised kidneys cannot handle the creatinine clearance load. If you have any kidney disease, get nephrology clearance first. Note: serum creatinine on a blood test will read 0.1–0.3 mg/dL higher than baseline; this is metabolic spillover, not injury. Inform your lab.
Should I take creatine with food?
It doesn't matter much. Some research suggests insulin co-administration improves uptake by ~10%, but the dose-saturation curve is sufficiently flat that this is functionally irrelevant at 3–5g/day. Take it when you'll actually remember to take it. With food helps if you have any GI sensitivity.
Is HCl, ethyl ester, or buffered creatine better than monohydrate?
No. Every "improved" creatine form on the market is more expensive than monohydrate and has not demonstrated superior performance in head-to-head studies. Ethyl ester is actually worse — it hydrolyzes to creatinine in the GI tract, lowering bioavailability. Stick with monohydrate; Creapure® (AlzChem) is the gold-standard supplier.
Can women take creatine?
Yes. The performance and lean-mass effects are well-replicated in female subjects. There is no hormonal mechanism by which creatine would behave differently. The "creatine is for men" notion is marketing, not biology. Same 3–5 g/day dose applies.
Does it help with brain function, sleep, or aging?
Maybe — at higher doses. Brain creatine pools are slower to saturate than muscle, and the dose-response curve points to 8–10 g/day for plausible cognitive effects. The signal is most consistent in sleep-deprived populations. For neuroprotection in aging, the evidence is suggestive but not yet A-grade. Watch our research feed; we re-grade quarterly.
How long does it take to "work"?
Performance gains are detectable around weeks 3–4 at 3–5 g/day maintenance dosing. Lean-mass changes lag slightly behind. If you load, you reach saturation in 5–7 days and detectable performance gains follow shortly after. Patience matters more than dose.
